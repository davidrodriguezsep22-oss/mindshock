03 — Final delivery

The ten reviewed pieces.

01
The delay before the toolPositioning

A repetitive task does not always deserve automation.

My first question is: where is the delay?

If nobody is waiting for a response, an approval, or a handoff, we may be automating out of enthusiasm rather than need.

Automation becomes useful when it removes a specific moment when the client or team is stuck.

Before choosing a tool, complete this sentence:

“Today we wait for ___ because ___.”

If you still cannot complete it clearly, you probably need to define the process before automating it.

02
The meeting ended without an ownerOperational tension

The meeting ended with several good ideas and no decision.

There was no shortage of information. What was missing was a clear answer about who would do what, and by when.

When a process depends on another meeting to remember the next step, the problem is usually not the agenda. It is the handoff.

Automation can remind, assign, or record that step. It cannot decide who accepts responsibility.

Technology accelerates clear agreements. It also accelerates confusion when the conversation ends without an owner or a date.

03
Three questions before automatingUtility

Three questions before automating a process:

1. What event starts the work?
2. Who has to make a decision?
3. What evidence confirms it is complete?

If one of those answers changes every week, do not start with the tool.

Start by agreeing on the process.

AI can classify, summarize, and propose. But if the team does not share a definition of “done,” it will probably produce work faster toward different destinations.

04
The hidden cost of an incomplete handoffEducation

An incomplete handoff forces the next person to rebuild the context.

A message can look clear and still leave three questions open:

— Who owns it?
— When is it needed?
— What does complete mean?

When those answers do not travel with the work, different versions of the same task appear.

Before automating the handoff, define the minimum information that must accompany it. Automating an incomplete message only distributes ambiguity faster.

05
What AI should not decide aloneTrust

Some decisions should not be closed by AI alone.

Especially when they affect a person, their reputation, or their access to an opportunity.

The tool can organize information, detect patterns, and suggest options. A sensitive decision needs a responsible person who understands the context and can explain the criterion.

Human review is not pressing “approve” at the end. It is preserving the ability to question, correct, and stop the process.

06
A process is not ready just because it has stepsPractical framework

A process is not ready for automation simply because someone wrote down its steps.

At a minimum, the team should be able to answer:

— What starts it?
— Who owns the result?
— Which rules repeat?
— Which exceptions require judgment?
— What information enters?
— How do we know it is complete?

When several answers are still open, the risk of automating an incomplete version of the work increases.

Test a small case first. Watch where an exception appears. Then decide which part deserves automation.

07
Visibility is not the same as efficiencyPositioning

A dashboard can make a process visible without making it more efficient.

Visibility matters: it reveals bottlenecks, workload, and delays. But showing ten blocked tasks does not decide which one should be resolved first.

If the team sees the problem and still does not know who decides, the dashboard is documenting a friction that has no rule yet.

Before automating an alert, ask: what decision should it trigger, and who has the authority to make it?

08
Human review must be able to stop the flowTrust

Human review matters when the person can change the outcome.

That requires three things:

— Time to review.
— Context to understand the decision.
— Authority to correct or stop the flow.

When review happens at the end, without information and without a real ability to intervene, it becomes a formality.

Designing supervision is not adding an approval. It is defining when and how a person can take control.

09
Exceptions reveal the real processEducation

Diagrams show the ideal process. Exceptions show the real one.

Before automating, observe what happens when a data point is missing, a priority changes, or an approval does not arrive.

If the system has no route for that case, someone will resolve it outside the flow and the record will be incomplete.

Not every exception should be automated. Some require human judgment. The important step is to identify them, assign an owner, and decide how they return to the normal flow.

10
Define “done” before buying another toolNarrative close

Before buying another tool, define what it means for the work to be done.

Sent?
Received?
Approved?
Resolved?

Each word creates a different process. It also changes which event an automation should record and which result the team should observe.

A tool cannot solve a definition the team does not yet share.

Choose one process this week and write one sentence: “This is done when ___.” Then check whether everyone completes the blank in the same way.
