01 — Voice profile

Concrete rules before writing.

The profile does not try to imitate a person through a vague label. It defines observable patterns the team can review, correct, and reuse.

Point of view

Practical and skeptical

Speaks from operations. Avoids presenting AI as a universal solution and asks which specific delay it removes.

Structure

Scene → tension → criterion

Opens with a recognizable situation, explains the problem, and ends with a useful decision—not a motivational line.

Rhythm

Short paragraphs

Alternates short sentences with a fuller explanation. One central idea per post.

Preferred language

Concrete and everyday

  • delay
  • handoff
  • decision
  • owner
  • friction
Language to avoid

Grand claims

  • revolutionary
  • disruptive
  • changes everything
  • guaranteed success
  • effortless
Editorial control

Never invent evidence

Figures, clients, results, and anecdotes are used only when the source has been approved by the team.

02 — Before and after

The difference is not more adjectives.

The adapted version keeps the core idea but turns it into a specific observation that follows the voice rules.

Generic draft

Artificial intelligence is revolutionizing the way companies work and enabling new levels of efficiency.

Problems: broad claim, interchangeable language, and no concrete situation.
Adapted to the voice

We do not automate a task because it is repetitive. We automate it when someone can point to the exact delay that disappears afterward.

Change: an operational criterion, clear contrast, and language that can be recognized and debated.

03 — Three LinkedIn drafts

Different pieces, one consistent voice.

These are review drafts, not automatic publications. The client confirms facts, boundaries, and changes before anything is used.

01OpinionA criterion for deciding what to automate.

A repetitive task does not always deserve automation. My first question is: where is the delay? If nobody is waiting for a response, an approval, or a handoff, we may be automating out of enthusiasm rather than need. The best automation is not the one with the most impressive demo. It is the one that removes a specific moment when the client or team is stuck. Before choosing a tool, write this sentence: “Today we wait for ___ because ___.” If you cannot complete it clearly, you do not yet have an automation case. You have an idea.

02Short storyMeetings that end without an owner.

The meeting ended with six good ideas and no decision. There was no shortage of information. There was no shortage of enthusiasm either. What was missing was a simple answer: who does what, and by when? When a process depends on another meeting to remember the next step, the problem is not the agenda. It is the handoff. Useful automation can remind, assign, or record that step. It cannot decide who accepts responsibility. Technology accelerates clear agreements. It also accelerates confusion when nobody closes the conversation.

03Practical frameworkEvaluate automation with three questions.

Three questions before automating a process: 1. What event starts the work? 2. Who has to make a decision? 3. What evidence confirms it is complete? If one of those answers changes every week, do not start with the tool. Start by agreeing on the process. AI can classify, summarize, and propose. But if the team does not share a definition of “done,” it will only produce work faster toward different destinations.
